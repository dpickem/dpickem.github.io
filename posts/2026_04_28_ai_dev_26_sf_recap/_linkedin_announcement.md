## LinkedIn post

I attended DeepLearning.AI's AI Dev 26 x SF this week and wrote up a fairly detailed recap - after all, without a recap, did you really attend the conference?

Overall, I want to point out two themes - one technical and one about the changing role of the software engineer.

First, agents are growing up.

Compared to earlier agentic AI conferences, this one felt much less focused on flashy demos and much more focused on the systems around agents that make them reliable enough to use in production (and at scale): safety boundaries, context and memory management, evals, observability, durable execution, sandboxing, and governance. In other words, the agent itself is only one part of the system. The harness, tools, policies, and feedback loops around it are becoming just as important.

Second, and this was a topic discussed in multiple talks and panels, the role of the software engineer is changing.

Writing code is no longer the primary activity; the job moves up a level of abstraction. This is also reflected in the traits and skill sets leaders in the field are looking for in their employees: systems thinking, curiosity, learning ability, domain depth, technical fluency, critical thinking, product/customer fluency, automation mindset, communication, and taste.

The junior-engineer story is the least settled part for me. Agents may let junior engineers take on harder work earlier, but they also threaten the old entry-level learning path where people built judgment and experience through junior roles at real companies. I have not heard a good answer about what to do about that hollowing out of the engineering talent pipeline where AI essentially removes the entry-level rung of the career ladder.

The phrase that stuck with me is something I have been thinking about a lot lately: "speed is only useful if you can close the loop." Faster implementation puts pressure on review, testing, CI, product, security, and operations. If those loops do not speed up as well, the bottleneck just moves somewhere else. And closing these longer and longer feedback loops is something I am becoming obsessed with...
